# Source Credibility Tiers
*Every claim used in an investigation gets a tier label before use. This is not optional.*

---

## The Tier System

| Tier | Source Type | Trust Level | Use as |
|------|------------|-------------|--------|
| **Tier 1** | Personal observed data (your sleep log) | Highest — N=1 but directly observed | Primary evidence |
| **Tier 2** | Peer-reviewed RCTs and meta-analyses | High — methodology determines weight | Interpretive framework |
| **Tier 3** | Clinical guidelines from professional bodies | High — consensus-based, practice-level | Baseline reference |
| **Tier 4** | Expert practitioners with disclosed methodology | Medium — check for Tier 2 backing before using | Starting hypothesis only |
| **Tier 5** | Popular science, wellness content, supplement marketing | Low — flag agenda before engaging | Do not use without verification |

---

## Cross-Cutting Credibility Heuristics

Use these signals when evaluating any source, regardless of tier:

| Signal | Higher credibility | Lower credibility |
|---|---|---|
| Sample size | Large, varied populations | Small, homogeneous |
| Measurement | PSG, actigraphy + subjective | Self-report only |
| Funding independence | NIH, foundation grants | Product company |
| Replication | Replicated across labs | Single study, own lab only |
| Language | "May," "in this study," "we found" | "Proven," "always," "destroys" |
| Conflict disclosure | Explicit, detailed | Absent or vague |
| Outcome type | Clinical (health, cognition) | Proxy (HRV, "sleep score") |

**Key framing distinction:** The biggest practical trap in sleep science is confusing *effect exists* (often true) with *effect size is clinically meaningful* (often overstated). A mechanism that is real under controlled lab conditions doesn't automatically mean the practical intervention produces meaningful change at real-world doses over real-world timescales. This gap between mechanistic clarity and clinical significance is where most genuine disagreements live.

## Tier 1 — Personal Observed Data

**What counts:** Anything the user has directly logged, tracked, or measured across multiple nights. Crash times, bed times, wake times, supplement timing, subjective ratings, mid-night wakes.

**Why it ranks highest:** It is specific to this person, this body, this context. Population studies average across thousands of people with different patterns. Your log is not averaged.

**Limitation:** Tier 1 data shows patterns and correlations. It cannot confirm mechanism. "I crash at 8pm every night" is Tier 1 observed fact. "I crash because of adenosine buildup" requires Tier 2 support.

**Minimum useful threshold:** 5 nights with full fields. Below that, patterns are suggestive, not reliable.

## Tier 2 — Peer-Reviewed Research

Real examples used by this researcher:

**Magnesium glycinate and sleep:**
- Rondanelli et al. (2021), *Nutrients* — meta-analysis of magnesium supplementation on sleep quality in older adults. Found statistically significant improvement on Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index. Note: most studies are in populations with magnesium deficiency; generalizability to replete adults is contested.
- Abbasi et al. (2012), *Journal of Research in Medical Sciences* — RCT, 46 elderly subjects, 500mg/day magnesium vs. placebo. Improved sleep efficiency, sleep time, and melatonin levels. Small sample; elderly population.
- PubMed PMC12412596 (2025) — randomized double-blind placebo-controlled trial, 155 adults aged 18–65, self-reported poor sleep. Magnesium bisglycinate vs. placebo.

**Tart cherry juice and melatonin:**
- Losso et al. (2018), *American Journal of Therapeutics* (PMC5617749) — pilot study, tart cherry juice increased melatonin intake by 85μg/day. Note: melatonin dose shown to treat insomnia ranges much higher. Effect size modest.
- Barforoush et al. (2025), *PMC12438961* — systematic review and meta-analysis of tart cherry on sleep quality and disorders. Found positive effects on sleep quality measures; most studies small.

**Sleep restriction therapy:**
- Pubmed 36709965 — comparison of sleep restriction vs. sleep compression. Both improved sleep continuity; sleep restriction showed faster early improvement. Note: protocol differences matter for real-world application.
- PMC12351270 — non-inferiority trial comparing sleep restriction therapy vs. sleep compression therapy for insomnia severity.

**Two-process model (Borbély):**
- Borbély (2022), *Journal of Sleep Research* (Pubmed 35502706) — original author's retrospective on the two-process model. Process S (homeostatic sleep pressure) + Process C (circadian drive) interaction.
- PMC9540767 — open-access version of Borbély's retrospective with full methodology.

**Sleep myths and evidence quality:**
- Robbins et al. (2019), *Sleep Health* (PMC6689426) — expert-identified sleep myths study. Cross-sectional evidence on common false beliefs. Useful for flagging Tier 5 claims.

## Tier 3 — Clinical Guidelines

**American Academy of Sleep Medicine (AASM):**
- URL: https://aasm.org/clinical-resources/practice-standards/practice-guidelines/
- Publishes clinical practice guidelines for diagnosis and treatment of sleep disorders. Consensus-based; requires multiple member votes and evidence review.
- Relevant: CBT-I (Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Insomnia) is the AASM first-line recommendation for chronic insomnia — not supplements, not medication.
- AASM does NOT endorse "sleep hygiene tips" as a standalone treatment for insomnia. This is a common misconception.

**Stanford Sleep Medicine:**
- Sleep restriction therapy protocol description: https://stanfordhealthcare.org/medical-treatments/c/cognitive-behavioral-therapy-insomnia/procedures/sleep-restriction.html
- Developed by Arthur Spielman. Used within CBT-I framework, not as standalone.

## Tier 4 — Expert Practitioners (with caveats)

### Matthew Walker — *Why We Sleep* (2017)

**Claim level:** Practitioner-expert with academic background (UC Berkeley sleep researcher). Author of the bestselling popular science book.

**Specific methodology caveats documented by Alexey Guzey (2019):**
- URL: https://guzey.com/books/why-we-sleep/
- Claim: "Shorter sleep = shorter life span" — Guzey found Walker misrepresented the cited epidemiological data. The source studies showed a U-curve, not linear decline; both too-little AND too-much sleep correlated with mortality. Walker graphed only half the curve.
- Claim: "WHO declared a global sleep loss epidemic" — Guzey could not find this WHO declaration. As of 2025, it remains uncited and unverifiable.
- Claim: "Two-thirds of adults in developed nations fail to get enough sleep" — Guzey traced this to a misread of a survey where "two-thirds" was the number who reported any sleep issue, not who slept under 7 hours nightly.
- Claim: "Sleeping less than 6 hours doubles your cancer risk" — Guzey found this was extrapolated from epidemiological correlations, not causal studies.
- Claim: "Lack of sleep can kill you within days" — Guzey challenged this using Fatal Familial Insomnia literature; the cause of death is prion disease, not sleep deprivation per se.
- Andrew Gelman (Columbia statistician, statmodeling.stat.columbia.edu) seconded Guzey's analysis publicly in 2019.

**Assessment:** Walker's core insight (sleep matters) is well-supported. The specific quantitative claims in the book frequently overstate the evidence. Use as a starting hypothesis generator, not a citation source.

### Andrew Huberman — Huberman Lab Podcast

**Claim level:** Neuroscientist (Stanford) with large public platform. Discloses some methodology; many supplement claims have mixed Tier 2 support.

**Supplement claims with evidence status:**
- Morning sunlight for circadian entrainment — **strong Tier 2 support** (zeitgeber research)
- Magnesium threonate for sleep — **weak Tier 2 support** (most RCTs used glycinate or citrate, not threonate specifically; threonate marketed for cognitive benefit)
- Apigenin (chamomile extract) for sleep — **very weak Tier 2** (mostly in vitro studies, one small human trial)
- Inositol for sleep — **weak Tier 2** (evidence primarily in mood disorders, not sleep architecture specifically)
- NSDR (non-sleep deep rest / yoga nidra) for recovery — **emerging Tier 2** (some studies on relaxation response; limited sleep-specific RCTs)

**URL:** https://www.hubermanlab.com/newsletter/improve-your-sleep

**Assessment:** Circadian protocols are generally well-grounded. Supplement stack extrapolates from weak or indirect evidence. Flag tier before engaging with any specific Huberman claim.

## Tier 5 — Popular Science / Wellness / Supplement Marketing

**Characteristics:** Undisclosed methodology, cherry-picked citations, commercial agenda, no peer review. Common in sleep content.

**Red flags:**
- Claims without study citations
- Study citations that don't say what the article claims
- Testimonials as evidence
- Product-specific claims ("our magnesium form is better than...")
- "Scientifically proven" language without naming the study

**Examples found in research:**
- Supplement product pages citing their own internal studies
- Wellness blogs citing Walker's book as a primary source (inheriting its caveats)
- YouTube content citing a single study as definitive

**Rule:** Do not engage with a Tier 5 claim on its own terms. First ask: is there Tier 2 support for the underlying claim?
