# Key Concepts
*Backbone definitions the researcher uses without hallucinating. Each concept is defined once, here, and cited when used in an investigation.*

## Sleep Architecture

**What it is:** The organized cycling of sleep stages across a night. Healthy sleep cycles through NREM and REM stages in approximately 90-minute rounds.

**NREM stages:**
- **N1 (light sleep):** Transition from wake to sleep. Easily disrupted. Hypnic jerks occur here.
- **N2 (intermediate sleep):** Body temperature drops, heart rate slows. Sleep spindles (bursts of neural activity) appear. Memory consolidation begins.
- **N3 (slow-wave / deep sleep):** Highest sleep pressure is discharged here. Most physically restorative. Growth hormone released. Hardest to wake from. Dominates early-night cycles.

**REM (rapid eye movement):**
- Emotional processing, memory consolidation (especially procedural and emotional memories).
- Vivid dreaming. Muscle atonia (paralysis of voluntary muscles — why you don't act out dreams).
- Dominates late-night cycles. The later you wake up, the more REM you cut.

**90-minute cycle:** Approximately 4-6 cycles per night. First cycles: mostly N3. Later cycles: mostly REM.

**Disruption relevance:** A mid-night or early-morning wake cuts into a REM-dominant phase. This explains why the feeling rating can be low even when total sleep is reasonable.

**Source basis:** Standard polysomnographic staging; AASM scoring manual.

## Adenosine System

**What it is:** Adenosine is a neurochemical byproduct of neural activity. It accumulates in the brain during waking hours, creating increasing "sleep pressure." As adenosine binds to receptors, alertness decreases.

**Clearance:** Adenosine is cleared during sleep — particularly during slow-wave (N3) sleep. This is what makes sleep restorative at the neurological level.

**Caffeine mechanism:** Caffeine works by blocking adenosine receptors (not by removing adenosine). When caffeine wears off, adenosine — which continued accumulating — binds rapidly, causing a crash. This is the caffeine crash mechanism. Caffeine does not reduce sleep pressure; it delays the perception of it.

**Evening crash relevance:** After a long waking day, adenosine is high. If waking began at ~9am and crash happens at ~8-9pm (11-12 hours of waking), accumulated sleep pressure is substantial. This is a predicted physiological state, not a malfunction.

**What the log can tell you:** Crash time minus wake time = hours awake. That number maps onto predicted adenosine load. If crash happens consistently at 11-12 hours of waking regardless of other variables, adenosine accumulation is a primary candidate.

**What the log cannot tell you:** Actual adenosine levels. Baseline individual sensitivity. Whether the crash is adenosine-primary vs. circadian-primary vs. postprandial.

**Source basis:** PMC9541543 (2022) — comprehensive review of adenosine, caffeine, and sleep-wake regulation.

## Circadian Rhythm

**What it is:** An internal ~24-hour biological clock driven by the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) in the hypothalamus. It regulates alertness, body temperature, cortisol release, melatonin production, and dozens of other biological processes.

**Zeitgebers ("time-givers"):** External signals that entrain (set) the circadian clock. Light is the strongest zeitgeber. Others: meal timing, exercise, social cues, temperature.

**Melatonin:** Released by the pineal gland ~2 hours before habitual sleep time. Signals biological night. It is a darkness signal, not a "sleep drug" — it does not cause sleep directly but shifts the circadian phase.

**Phase advance/delay:**
- **Phase advance:** Circadian clock shifts earlier (earlier sleep and wake times).
- **Phase delay:** Circadian clock shifts later (later sleep and wake times).
- Evening light exposure → phase delay (pushes bedtime later). Morning light → phase advance (pulls wake time and bedtime earlier).

**Cortisol rhythm:** Peaks within 30-45 minutes of waking (cortisol awakening response). Declines through the day. Evening cortisol is low — alertness in the evening is primarily maintained by circadian drive and adenosine resistance, not cortisol.

**Relevance to Roc's pattern:** Roc's work ending at ~5:30-6pm followed by a crash at 8-9pm is consistent with the circadian window approaching biological night. The circadian drive for wakefulness wanes in the evening as melatonin onset approaches. This is predicted.

**Source basis:** Borbély (2022), Pubmed 35502706; standard circadian biology literature.

## Two-Process Model — Borbély (1982, revised 2022)

**What it is:** The dominant framework for understanding sleep timing. Sleep is regulated by the interaction of two independent processes:

- **Process S (homeostatic sleep pressure):** Builds during waking, dissipates during sleep. Adenosine is the primary biochemical substrate. After a poor night, Process S is higher at any given waking hour (more sleep debt). After a good night, lower.

- **Process C (circadian drive):** The 24-hour clock-driven variation in alertness and sleep propensity. It alternates between promoting wakefulness (mid-morning to late afternoon) and sleep (nighttime, with a secondary dip in the early afternoon).

**The interaction:** Sleep occurs when Process S (pressure) is high AND Process C (circadian timing) permits it. This is why you can be exhausted (high S) but still struggle to sleep at 3pm (C promoting wakefulness) — and why you crash at 8pm when both S is high and C begins its nighttime phase.

**Relevance to Roc's crash-to-bed window:** The crash (Process S overwhelming C) happens at 8-9pm. The large gap to bed (2-4 hours later) means Roc is pushing against high S with sustained C — which is why going to bed earlier produces longer sleep totals. Earlier bed time catches the high-S window before C shifts.

**Source basis:** Borbély AA (2022) *Journal of Sleep Research* Pubmed 35502706; open-access version PMC9540767.

## Postprandial Dip

**What it is:** An energy drop that occurs approximately 1-3 hours after eating, driven by:
1. Parasympathetic activation post-meal (body shifts resources to digestion)
2. Insulin-driven glucose regulation (blood sugar response)
3. Circadian phase interaction (the natural circadian dip in the early-to-mid afternoon is often confused with or amplified by postprandial effects)

**Timing:** The "afternoon slump" (2-3pm) is partly circadian (predicted by Process C) and partly postprandial (if lunch was eaten 1-2 hours prior). These overlap and reinforce each other.

**Evening relevance:** A large meal in the early evening (5-7pm) can produce a postprandial dip 1-3 hours later — landing squarely at 7-9pm. This interacts with rising adenosine and the circadian transition toward sleep.

**Roc's log context:** Phase 2 intervention is a walk after dinner (~7:30pm). The mechanism hypothesis is that walking blunts the postprandial dip by activating sympathetic nervous system, delaying the crash. On May 28 (no walk, plugin session instead) — crash at 9:10pm. On May 29 (outdoor dinner/walk) — crash at 8:00pm. One-night comparison; not yet a pattern.

**What the log cannot tell you:** Whether the crash on walk nights vs. non-walk nights is due to postprandial blunting, or dinner timing, or fatigue level, or something else. Controlled comparison requires more nights with isolated variables.

**Source basis:** General circadian biology; HMN24 circadian dip literature; postprandial physiology.

## Crash-to-Bed Window

**What it is:** A derived variable specific to Roc's log. The gap (in hours) between the recorded crash time and the actual time in bed.

**How to calculate:** Bed time − Crash time = crash-to-bed window. If crash is 8:00pm and bed is 2:14am, the window is 6h14m.

**Why it matters:** This window is where the most investigation-relevant behavior happens. During this gap:
- Process S (sleep pressure) is high
- Body is biologically ready for sleep
- The person is awake anyway — doing something

A large crash-to-bed window indicates a consistent mismatch between biological sleep readiness and actual sleep behavior. The log shows this is Roc's primary pattern variable.

**Roc's observed windows:**
- May 26→27: Crash 8:46pm, Bed 3:40am → **6h54m window**
- May 27→28: Crash 9:10pm, Bed 2:40am → **5h30m window**
- May 28→29: Crash 9:10pm, Bed 1:40am → **4h30m window**
- May 29→30: Crash 8:00pm, Bed 2:14am → **6h14m window**

**Pattern visible in log:** Total sleep correlates with bed time, not crash time. Earlier bed → longer total. The crash time is relatively stable (8-9pm). The variable is when Roc actually gets into bed.

**What this is not:** This is not a diagnosis of delayed sleep phase disorder (DSPD), which is a circadian misalignment condition. The crash is early; the delay is behavioral, not circadian. A DSPD pattern would show no crash until late night with a late natural wake. Roc's pattern is the opposite.
