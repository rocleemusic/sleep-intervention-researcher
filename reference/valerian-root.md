# Valerian Root: Sleep Research Summary

## Overview

Valerian (*Valeriana officinalis*) is the most commonly used herbal sleep supplement in both the US and Europe. The research base consists of multiple RCTs, two major meta-analyses, and a 2020 narrative review. The evidence is genuinely mixed — positive subjective signals, negative objective signals, with significant methodological problems throughout.

## What the Research Found

### The Positive Signal — and Why It's Fragile

Bent et al. pooled 6 studies reporting a dichotomous sleep quality outcome ("sleep improved or not") and found valerian nearly doubled the odds of reporting improved sleep:

- **Relative risk = 1.8 (95% CI: 1.2–2.9)**

However, the same analysis detected **statistically significant publication bias** (Kendall's tau, p < 0.01), meaning small negative studies were likely unpublished. The summary estimate is probably inflated.

Fernández-San-Martín et al. (2010) reached a similar conclusion in their meta-analysis: valerian showed subjective improvement in insomnia, but the effect was not demonstrated in quantitative or objective measures.

The 2020 JEBIM review was the most skeptical: despite classifying valerian as a safe herb, the authors concluded evidence did **not** support clinical efficacy for insomnia.

### The Objective Measurement Problem

Five of the 16 RCTs in Bent et al. used **polysomnography (PSG)** — the gold-standard objective sleep measurement. Across all five, there were **no consistent, statistically significant changes** in any objective measure:

- Sleep efficiency index
- Sleep period time
- Time in each sleep stage (including REM and SWS)
- Measured sleep onset latency
- REM onset latency
- Number of arousals

The pattern across all reviews: people *feel* like they slept better; the brainwave recordings show nothing changed. This subjective/objective dissociation is the defining feature of the valerian evidence base.

### Sleep Onset Latency

Nine studies measured time to fall asleep. Four reported results in minutes:

| Study | Result | Significant? |
|---|---|---|
| Farag (2003), n=25 | 16.7 min faster | Yes (p=0.003) |
| Balderer (1985), n=10 | 9–13.5 min faster | Yes (p<0.01) |
| Donath (2000), n=16 | 15 min faster | No |
| Francis (2002), n=5 | 17.7 min faster | No |

Cannot pool — data presentation was too variable across studies. Half significant, half not.

### Hangover Effect

Consistently **none**, across all 6 studies that measured morning alertness. No difference between valerian and placebo groups. This is a genuine advantage over OTC antihistamine sleep aids (diphenhydramine), which reduce slow-wave sleep and cause tolerance rapidly.

## Why the Evidence Is Weak

### 1. Standardization Problem (Most Significant)

Valerenic acid is the suspected primary active compound. Across 16 RCTs in Bent et al.:

- Doses ranged from **225mg to 1215mg/day** (a 5x range)
- Only **2 of 16 studies** specified the extract was standardized to a valerenic acid percentage
- Commercial US products ranged from **75mg to 3000mg** recommended daily (40x range)
- Most standardized products used 0.8% valerenic acids, but this was rarely confirmed in trials

Studies cannot be meaningfully compared when the actual active compound dosing is this variable.

### 2. Most Single-Dose Studies Show Nothing

Positive results tended to come from multi-week trials. Studies testing a single dose found no significant difference from placebo. This suggests any effect (if real) requires cumulative dosing.

### 3. Methodological Quality

- Average Jadad score: 3.4/5 across Bent et al. studies
- 8 of 16 studies enrolled fewer than 25 participants
- No standardized outcome measure across studies — made pooling impossible for most endpoints
- Adverse events inconsistently assessed and reported

### 4. Heterogeneous Populations

Studies enrolled: healthy volunteers, people with self-reported sleep problems, elderly inpatients, children with intellectual deficits, anxiety patients. These populations respond differently and cannot be meaningfully aggregated.

---

## Proposed Mechanism

Valerenic acid modulates **GABA-A receptors** — the same pathway as benzodiazepines, but considerably weaker. Also shows some **adenosine receptor** activity. The mechanism is biologically plausible (more so than many supplements), but plausible mechanism does not confirm clinical effect. The PSG data consistently failing to show change suggests either the mechanism is too weak at typical doses, or only subjective perception is affected.

---

## Safety

**Short-term (≤4 weeks):** Generally well-tolerated. One large study (Jacobs, n=270) found diarrhea in 18% of valerian group vs. 8% placebo (p=0.02) at 600mg.

**Hepatotoxicity:** Rare case reports exist. Causation is difficult to establish given polypharmacy in reported cases. Not observed in any of the 16 RCTs. Long-term safety data is limited — most trials ran ≤1 month.

**Tolerance/withdrawal:** Not adequately studied. No RCTs designed to assess this. Cannot rule out.

**Drug interactions:** Potential additive CNS depression with alcohol, sedatives, anxiolytics. Theoretical CYP3A4 interaction (not well characterized).

|---|---|---|

| Improves objective sleep (PSG)? | No consistent evidence | High (consistently negative) |
| Reduces sleep onset latency? | Inconsistent (~15 min in some studies) | Low |
| Causes morning grogginess? | No | Moderate-high |
| GABA-A mechanism plausible? | Yes | Moderate |
| Safe short-term? | Appears so | Moderate |
| Product delivers active compound? | Unknown — standardization is poor | Low |

## Research Gaps

- No large, high-quality RCT using standardized extract and PSG endpoints exists
- No head-to-head comparison with low-dose melatonin, magnesium, or CBT-I
- Long-term safety and tolerance data absent
- No data on which subpopulations (sleep onset vs. maintenance insomnia, anxious vs. non-anxious) might respond differently
- Optimal dose and duration unknown

---

## Practical Framing

Valerian is probably not inert for some people, but the objective signal is weak enough that if it feels helpful, the experience is real — but you cannot confirm whether you're responding to the compound, the ritual, or regression to the mean on a bad-sleep night. The no-hangover profile is its clearest documented advantage over OTC alternatives. For anyone with a clinically significant sleep disorder, it is not a substitute for assessment and evidence-based treatment.
